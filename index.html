cUTI Antibiotic Duration Decision Aid

For adults with complicated urinary tract infection or acute pyelonephritis: assess suitability for a short course versus a longer, individualized course.

Prototype only. This is a transparent, rule-based educational aid—not a validated prediction score. It does not replace clinical judgment, microbiology, imaging, source control, local guidelines, or specialist consultation.
0 of 8
1. Is this an adult with cUTI, febrile UTI, or acute pyelonephritis?
Do not use for asymptomatic bacteriuria or uncomplicated cystitis.
2. Has effective antimicrobial therapy been established?
The agent is active against the pathogen and reaches adequate levels in urine and relevant tissue; bloodstream exposure is also adequate if bacteremia is present.
3. Is the patient improving clinically within 48–72 hours?
Consider fever, urinary/systemic symptoms, organ function, and overall trajectory.
4. Is the patient hemodynamically stable?
No ongoing shock or persistent clinical instability.
5. Has adequate source control been achieved?
No unresolved complete obstruction, undrained renal/perinephric abscess, infected obstructing stone, or other persistent nidus.
6. Is acute bacterial prostatitis suspected?
Compatible pelvic/perineal symptoms, examination, bacteremia pattern, or other clinical concern.
7. Is there a major evidence-gap or high-risk feature?
Examples include severe immunocompromise, pregnancy, severe renal insufficiency, recent urologic surgery, indwelling urinary devices, or major structural/functional urinary abnormalities.
8. Is the selected antibiotic, dose, and route adequate for the intended short course?
Short-course evidence is strongest for fluoroquinolones; evidence for short oral beta-lactam courses is less certain and optimized dosing may be required.

Interpretation notes

  • A short-course result means short-course eligible, not guaranteed cure.
  • When eligible, consider 5–7 days for a fluoroquinolone or 7 days for another effective agent, counted from the first day of effective therapy.
  • Associated uncomplicated Gram-negative bacteremia alone does not automatically require 14 days when effective therapy, improvement, stability, and source control are present.
  • Failure to improve should trigger evaluation for an ongoing nidus, inadequate antimicrobial activity or exposure, and an alternative diagnosis—not merely automatic extension.
